A Calendar of Color

Vibrant Festivals

Witness the harmony of a nation that respects and celebrates the traditions of all its people.

Thaipusam

A staggering display of devotion observed by the Tamil Hindu community on the full moon of the Tamil month of Thai. Devotees carry elaborate "Kavadis" (burdens) pierced into their skin as they march towards the temples, most famously ascending the steps of Batu Caves.

Deepavali

Known as the Festival of Lights, Deepavali celebrates the spiritual victory of light over darkness. Homes are illuminated with oil lamps (diyas), adorned with intricate colored powder designs (kolam) at the entrances, and families gather for feasts and prayers.

Gawai Dayak

An annual harvest festival enthusiastically celebrated by the Dayak people in Sarawak. It marks the end of the paddy harvesting season with traditional longhouse open doors, the drinking of tuak (rice wine), the Ngajat dance, and expressions of gratitude to the spirits.

Kaamatan

Celebrated primarily in Sabah by the Kadazan-Dusun and related ethnic groups, this is the month-long harvest festival culminating in a grand finale. It honors Bambaazon (the rice spirit) and features the traditional Sumazau dance and the crowning of the Unduk Ngadau (harvest beauty queen).

Chinese New Year

A roaring 15-day celebration marking the beginning of the lunar calendar year. Streets turn red with lanterns, families reunite for massive dinners, elders give out ang pows (red packets for luck), and lively lion and dragon dances sweep out bad spirits.

Mid-Autumn Festival

Often referred to as the Lantern Festival or Mooncake Festival, it celebrates the harvest and the brightest moon of the year. Families gather in the evening to light paper lanterns and share exquisite, deeply symbolic mooncakes.

Qingming Festival

Known as Tomb-Sweeping Day, this is an important time for filial piety. Families visit the graves of their ancestors to clean the tombstones, offer food, and burn joss paper (spirit money) to ensure their ancestors are well-provided for in the afterlife.

Chap Goh Mei

Falling on the 15th and final night of Chinese New Year, this festival acts as a vibrant conclusion to the celebrations. Historically famous for young, unmarried women throwing Mandarin oranges into seas or rivers hoping to find a good husband.

Hari Raya Aidilfitri

Marking the end of the fasting month of Ramadan, this triumphant day sees Muslims returning to their hometowns (balik kampung). The air fills with the aroma of rendang and lemang, while open houses welcome neighbors of all races to feast in forgiveness.
